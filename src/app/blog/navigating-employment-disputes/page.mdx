Employment disputes can arise in many forms, from disagreements over compensation to wrongful termination claims. Understanding how these disputes typically unfold—and how to navigate them—can help you protect your interests and achieve a fair resolution.

## Common Types of Employment Disputes

### Wrongful Termination

Wrongful termination occurs when an employer fires an employee in violation of legal protections. This may include:

- Termination based on discrimination
- Retaliation for whistleblowing or filing complaints
- Violation of employment contract terms
- Termination that violates public policy

### Wage and Hour Disputes

These disputes involve disagreements over:

- Unpaid overtime
- Minimum wage violations
- Misclassification as exempt employees
- Unpaid commissions or bonuses
- Illegal wage deductions

### Breach of Contract

When either party fails to uphold the terms of an employment agreement, a breach of contract claim may arise. This includes violations of:

- Written employment contracts
- Non-compete agreements
- Severance agreements
- Confidentiality agreements

## Resolution Options

### Informal Resolution

Many employment disputes can be resolved through direct communication with your employer. This approach works best when:

- The issue is relatively minor
- You have a good relationship with management
- The employer is willing to address your concerns

### Mediation

Mediation involves a neutral third party who helps both sides reach a voluntary agreement. Benefits include:

- Lower cost than litigation
- Faster resolution
- More control over the outcome
- Confidential proceedings
- Preserved relationships

### Arbitration

Many employment contracts include mandatory arbitration clauses. Arbitration is similar to a trial but less formal:

- A neutral arbitrator makes binding decisions
- Limited discovery and appeals
- Often faster and less expensive than court
- May limit your legal options

### Litigation

When other options fail, litigation may be necessary. This involves:

- Filing a lawsuit in court
- Formal discovery process
- Potential trial before a judge or jury
- Appeals process available

## Building Your Case

Regardless of which resolution path you pursue, preparation is essential:

### Gather Evidence

- Employment contracts and offer letters
- Performance reviews and evaluations
- Relevant emails and communications
- Pay stubs and compensation records
- Witness statements

### Know the Timeline

Employment claims often have strict deadlines:

- EEOC charges must typically be filed within 180-300 days
- State claims may have different deadlines
- Contract claims have statute of limitations periods

### Calculate Your Damages

Understanding what you've lost helps in negotiations:

- Lost wages and benefits
- Emotional distress
- Future earning capacity
- Legal fees and costs

## When to Seek Legal Help

Consider consulting an employment attorney when:

- You're unsure of your legal rights
- The dispute involves significant money or your career
- You're facing retaliation
- Your employer has legal representation
- Deadlines are approaching

An experienced attorney can evaluate your case, explain your options, and advocate for your interests throughout the resolution process.
